Abstract

Despite significant advances in autonomous navigation, learning complex steering behaviors that adapt to diverse environments remains a fundamental challenge. This is crucial for various applications from urban driving to off-road exploration and precision robotics. Generating adaptive steering behaviors is fundamentally challenging because the learning system needs to maintain separate representations for different steering scenarios, even when they share similar characteristics, and then carry out global optimization during execution.

To address adaptive steering generation, we first identify features within the learning model that can carry steering behavior information. We then use them to separate and classify different steering scenarios during the learning process and detect under-performance and over-fitting. We fix the latter by training a model that proposes optimal steering parameters for new scenarios, based on the learned behaviors from existing ones, and show how it can be used to guide autonomous navigation with natural steering control.

Our approach, Learn to Steer, does not depend on external sources to determine steering behavior, but rather uses the prior from the learning model itself, creating environment-dependent and task-dependent steering strategies. Evaluated on multiple benchmark datasets, we find that Learn to Steer strongly outperforms the steering accuracy of existing baselines.

How does Learn to Steer work?

Architecture of Learn to Steer

Our method, Learn to Steer, aims to enhance autonomous navigation systems to accurately produce intended steering behaviors for complex environmental scenarios. Our methodology involves a two-step process: initially, we learn natural steering patterns that specify how and when steering adjustments should occur. These patterns emerge naturally from the data-driven learning process. At the second step, we use these patterns as a blueprint to generate optimal steering commands.

Identify "Steering Behavior" in Learning Models

While most layers do not exhibit separability at the behavior level, we notice that specific layers at specific timestamps tend to generate different features for different steering scenarios. Based on this finding, we select the attention features to serve as our behavior-level features.

ReSteer - Natural Behavior Correction

To address under-performance issues, we train a reinforcement learning model to predict optimal steering parameters, represented as multi-dimensional control signals. To train our ReSteer model, we need a dataset of steering behavior pairs with similar and different scenarios that maintain the same environmental context.

We begin with the empirical observation that slight variations in the steering parameters specified in the training data—while keeping the environment and task consistent—typically results in similar steering behaviors. This consistency is crucial as it allows us to generate a training dataset of behavior pairs where each pair has similar steering characteristics, differing by only the control parameters, thereby preserving the overall navigation strategy.

Qualitative Results

We evaluated Learn to Steer against traditional PID control, Model Predictive Control, and other learning-based approaches. Our method successfully generates natural steering behaviors, while other methods struggle in some or all of the examples.
